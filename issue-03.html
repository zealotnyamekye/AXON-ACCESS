What is it

Memory Is Not One Thing. It Is a Process.

Memory is not stored like a file on a hard drive. It is reconstructed every time you recall something — which is part of why it is so unreliable and so fascinating. There are three key stages: encoding (taking in new information), storage (holding it over time), and retrieval (calling it back up when you need it).

The brain region most central to memory is the hippocampus — it acts like a filing clerk, deciding what gets sent to long-term storage while you sleep.

Short-term memory holds a small amount of information briefly. Long-term memory stores facts, experiences, and skills over years. And working memory is what you use right now to hold ideas while thinking through a problem.

Why You Should Care

Forgetting Is Not a Flaw. But Some Forgetting Can Be Prevented.

Forgetting happens for several reasons. Sometimes information was never properly encoded — often because of distraction during learning. Other times, memories fade through a process called decay when they are not revisited. And sometimes newer information interferes with older memories, a process called interference.

But here is what makes this relevant to you right now. Two of the biggest factors in how well your memory works are completely within your control.

The Two Big Levers

What you eat and how much you sleep directly shape your memory

Food: Your brain is about 60% fat. Omega-3 fatty acids (found in fish, walnuts, and flaxseed) strengthen memory. Antioxidants protect neurons from damage. High-sugar diets and ultra-processed foods have been linked to reduced hippocampal volume over time.

Sleep: During deep sleep, the hippocampus replays the day's experiences and transfers them into long-term storage — a process called memory consolidation. Studying all night then sleeping only three hours is one of the least effective revision strategies, because the brain never gets the chance to lock in what you learned.

→ Both are within your control
Cool Fact · Mind = Blown

You Can Strengthen a Memory Every Time You Recall It

Every time you retrieve a memory, you actually make it stronger. This is the science behind a study technique called retrieval practice — or the testing effect.

Instead of rereading your notes, actively testing yourself forces your brain to reconstruct the memory, reinforcing the neural pathway each time. Research shows this produces far better long-term retention than passive review. Your brain literally rewires itself to remember things you practise recalling.
Myth Buster

Let's Set the Record Straight

✕ The Myth

"I just have a bad memory. Some people are born with better memories than others and there is nothing I can do about it."

✓ The Truth

Memory is a skill that responds to how you treat your brain. Sleep, nutrition, stress levels, and study habits all directly shape how well your memory works. Neuroplasticity means your brain can build stronger memory networks at any age — including right now as a teenager.

Try This Today

The Brain-Friendly Study Method

Try this approach the next time you have something to study. It is built directly on memory science and takes under 30 minutes.

1
Eat before you study. A small meal with protein and complex carbs — like eggs and whole grain bread, or groundnut soup and rice — fuels your hippocampus.
2
Read your notes once. Then close the book. Write down everything you can remember from scratch. Do not peek. This is retrieval practice in action.
3
Check only what you missed. Re-read the gaps, not the whole thing.
4
Space it out. Review the material again after 24 hours, then again after three days. Spaced repetition is one of the most powerful memory tools in neuroscience.
5
Sleep. Get at least 8 hours after studying. This is when your hippocampus does the real work of locking in what you learned.
💡

Why it works: This method combines three evidence-based strategies — retrieval practice, spaced repetition, and sleep consolidation. Each targets a different stage of memory formation. Together, they work with your brain's natural biology rather than against it.
